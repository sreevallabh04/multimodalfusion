We thank the reviewer for highlighting this important inconsistency. The AUC value of 0.25
reported in the earlier version of Figure 12 was due to an error in the plotting/labeling of the

confirms that the model performs substantially better than random prediction. The discrepancy
has been resolved, and both the figure and the corresponding description have been updated
to ensure consistency and clarity.

Reviewer 1: In some experiments, perfect accuracy rates of 99.99% were achieved. This
raises the question of whether different leaf images from the same plant are distributed
between the training and validation sets during data separation. Please clarify the data
separation.
Author’s response:
We thank the reviewer for highlighting this important concern regarding potential data leakage,
particularly in light of the high accuracy values reported. We would like to clarify that data
separation in this study was performed strictly at the plant level, not at the individual image
level. All leaf images originating from the same coffee plant were assigned exclusively to either
the training or the validation set and were never shared between them. As a result, the
validation set consisted entirely of images from previously unseen plants, ensuring a fair and
unbiased evaluation of the models’ generalization capability. This plant-level separation
strategy was applied consistently across both the standard 80/20 train–validation split and the
K-fold cross-validation experiments. Therefore, the high accuracy values observed are not
attributable to overlap between training and validation data, but reflect the effectiveness of the
proposed preprocessing pipeline, feature extraction strategy, and model architectures. This
clarification has now been explicitly added into section 4.2 of the revised manuscript to
improve transparency and reproducibility.

Reviewer 1: Why did you run your study for only 10 epochs? It is recommended to run it for
100 epochs and show the results.
Author’s response:
We thank the reviewer for the suggestion to extend training to 100 epochs. Accordingly, we
conducted additional experiments training the model initially up to 25 epochs. The results show
rapid convergence: both training and validation accuracy exceed 99% within the first 5–8
epochs, while validation loss reaches a minimum and stabilizes by approximately the 8th–10th
epoch. Beyond this point, further training yields negligible or no statistically significant
improvements, with occasional fluctuations in validation loss, indicating performance
saturation rather than continued learning. Therefore, limiting training to 10 epochs represents
an empirically justified and computationally efficient choice. We have also attached the
accuracy curve for 25 epochs for your reference.
